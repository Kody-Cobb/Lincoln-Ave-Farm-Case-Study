02 Strategic Rationale — Why Disaggregation Creates Value

A whole-farm sale prices the nut acreage discount into every acre across the entire 1,190 tillable acres. A sophisticated buyer acquiring 1,300 acres of mixed California permanent crop in MAGSA — groundwater only, SGMA ramp-down underway — will apply a blended discount to the entire property that reflects the worst-water-risk acreage. The peach block and the open ground effectively subsidize the buyer's risk on the nut acreage. Disaggregating the property into three separately marketed parcels exposes each acre to its own natural buyer pool, each of whom values that specific use case without applying a discount driven by someone else's crop.

Parcel A — Nut Block (Almonds + Pistachios + Figs · 473 acres) · Sell Now
The nut block is the most water-sensitive and time-sensitive piece. Almonds require 3.5–4.0 AF/acre. Pistachios require 3.0–3.5 AF/acre. The current GSP sustainable yield of 1.16 AF/acre already cannot support commercial nut production without deficit irrigation. The draft Allocation Framework's 0.72 AF/acre preliminary figure makes it untenable. The Nut & Fig Tenant's lease runs through September 2032 — that in-place income is the asset. Sell while a tenant is paying rent and a buyer can underwrite to that income. Every year of delay brings the 2030 cumulative 30% reduction closer and compresses the buyer pool. The right acquirer is a nut crop operator who believes they can manage the water constraints or plans to renegotiate the lease terms — IFC does not need to solve the water problem, only to sell before the market prices it as fully impaired.
Parcel B — Peach Block (251 acres) · Hold Through 2029, Sell 2028–29
Peaches require 2.5–3.5 AF/acre — meaningfully less than nuts. The gap between crop water requirements and available allocation is smaller, and stone fruit operators have more flexibility to manage deficit irrigation in a mature orchard than nut growers do. More importantly, the lease structure is compelling: fixed at $700/net acre with 2% annual compounding escalation, expiring December 2029. That escalation compounds to approximately $789/acre in year six — on 251 acres, nearly $200,000 per year in contractually escalating income. Hold through the lease, collect the compounding rent, and execute a disciplined sale in 2028 as the lease approaches expiry with a producing orchard still in the ground. A 2028 sale into a stone fruit buyer who can re-lease or owner-operate is a better outcome than including this acreage in a blended whole-farm sale that discounts it for nut-related water risk it does not carry.
Parcel C — Open Block (~390 acres) · Convert to Alternative Use
The open ground generating $150/acre in barley rent is the most underutilized value in the portfolio and the clearest illustration of Ralph's "sum of parts" thesis. Solar ground leases in Fresno County run $800–1,500/acre annually on 25–35 year terms with creditworthy utility or developer counterparties. That is 5–10x the current rent, and solar is completely indifferent to SGMA — photovoltaic panels do not require groundwater. A solar conversion simultaneously eliminates water risk as a pricing factor and multiplies income. Conservation easement payments from land trusts or USDA ACEP programs are a second path, converting agricultural land value into a one-time payment that can exceed the depressed market value of the parcels outright. Battery energy storage system (BESS) leasing is an emerging third alternative with comparable economics. IFC has already executed this playbook — the Great Canal conservation easement in California was nominated for Agri Investor Deal of Year. Lincoln Ave's open ground is the same move applied to a different context.

02b Downside Cases — What Has to Go Wrong
The disaggregation strategy is not without execution risk. Each scenario below is specific, quantified where possible, and answered honestly. IC should evaluate whether these risks are acceptable before approving the recommended path.
Parcel C — Solar / conversion fails
Medium-high probability if not pre-screened
The risk
Solar conversion requires viable grid interconnection. CAISO transmission queues in the San Joaquin Valley are among the most congested in California. If the site is too far from existing substation capacity, no solar developer will lease it. Conservation easement is the fallback but generates lower proceeds. If both fail, the open ground reverts to status quo: $150/ac barley rent indefinitely.
Quantified impact
Solar net proceeds (base case): ~$7M. Conservation easement fallback: ~$3–4M. Status quo value (capitalized barley rent): ~$0.9M. Swing of $3–6M on portfolio total proceeds. Portfolio IRR drops from ~3–4% to ~1–2% if solar fails and conservation easement also unavailable. This is the single largest risk to the disaggregation strategy.
Mitigation
Commission a CAISO interconnection pre-screen and GIS proximity analysis before marketing Parcel C as a solar candidate. This is a 30-day, sub-$25K exercise that either validates the solar path or eliminates it early. Simultaneously initiate land trust conversations — conservation easement does not require interconnection and can proceed in parallel. Do not assume solar is viable; confirm it.
Parcel A — SGMA restrictions accelerate / nut buyers disappear
Moderate probability — market deteriorating
The risk
The Kings Subbasin submitted updated GSPs to DWR in January 2025 addressing 12 corrective actions. If DWR determines implementation is off-track in its periodic review, the subbasin risks probation and state intervention — which would accelerate restrictions and likely trigger immediate buyer repricing. Separately, if almond and pistachio commodity prices remain depressed, the flex rent true-up on the Nut & Fig Tenant lease falls further, compressing the cap-rate-implied value and narrowing the buyer pool to land-value-only bidders.
Quantified impact
If exit cap rate blows out to 10% (vs. 7% base) and flex rent falls to 70% of estimate, Parcel A blended value falls from ~$7.7M to ~$4.5M. ~$3.2M reduction in proceeds. If MAGSA is placed on probation and state intervention triggers immediate allocation curtailment, white-land nut orchard values in comparable subbasins have declined 30–40% in 12 months. At 35% additional compression, Parcel A proceeds could fall to ~$3M.
Mitigation
Urgency of the Parcel A sale is the mitigation. Every quarter of delay brings DWR's next periodic review closer and adds SGMA implementation risk to the buyer's underwriting. The sell-now recommendation exists precisely because the window where a nut lease buyer will pay for in-place income is finite. The correct response to this risk is to move faster on Parcel A, not to reconsider the strategy. Commission a BOV immediately. Do not wait for the CEO meeting to initiate the marketing process.
Parcel B — Peach tenant water-stressed / 2028 sale window misses
Lower probability but not negligible
The risk
Stone fruit requires 3.2 AF/ac. Current GSP sustainable yield is 1.16 AF/ac. The tenant is deficit-irrigating today. If the MAGSA 10% cumulative reduction (already underway in 2025) materially degrades peach yields, the tenant may seek rent abatement, decline to renew, or walk the lease. IFC holds a NN lease, meaning any unexpected maintenance event — irrigation failure, frost damage, pump replacement — hits IFC's P&L directly. The 2024 NOI crater ($449K vs. $888K in 2023) is proof this exposure is real.
Quantified impact
If a $200K maintenance event hits in 2027 (comparable to 2024), Parcel B cumulative rent 2026–2028 falls from ~$550K to ~$350K. If the tenant vacates and IFC sells to a land buyer (not an operator) in 2029, exit land value falls from $12K/ac (producing orchard, sitting tenant) to $8–9K/ac (vacant ground). Combined downside: ~$1.5M reduction in Parcel B proceeds.
Mitigation
Commission an agronomic assessment of the peach block in Q3 2026 — orchard health, rootstock age, irrigation system condition, and water usage history. If the orchard is aging or the tenant shows signs of stress, accelerate the Parcel B marketing timeline to 2027 rather than 2028. Accept a slightly lower exit multiple in exchange for executing before a potential vacancy event. Holding a NN lease through a deteriorating water environment is the risk; the mitigation is moving the exit earlier.

Nut Block premium

The largest single driver. A whole-farm buyer values open ground at the blended agricultural rate — $5K–$9K/ac. Solar or conservation conversion monetizes it at $10K–$20K/ac equivalent. The conversion itself creates value before any sale occurs.

Why disaggregation beats whole-farm sale

A single buyer for all 1,300 acres prices the nut block's water impairment into every acre — including the peaches and the open ground. Disaggregating exposes each acre to its own buyer pool. The dedicated nut crop buyer prices the in-place lease through 2032. The solar developer prices the open ground on energy economics, not agricultural water availability. The stone fruit operator prices the peach block on lease economics and orchard productivity, not SGMA trajectory.

The portfolio IRR above is the honest number. IFC paid $14,252/ac at peak in June 2022. The disaggregation strategy does not fully recover the basis — the nut block has been structurally impaired by SGMA. What it does is maximize recovery within that constraint, with the Parcel C conversion doing the heaviest lifting on total return.

04 Due Diligence, Knowledge Gaps & Next Steps
Lease structure glossary — NN vs. NNN
Gross
Landlord pays all
Tenant pays rent only. Landlord absorbs taxes, insurance, and all maintenance. Maximum exposure for IFC; most favorable to tenant.
Net (N)
Tenant pays taxes
Tenant pays property taxes. IFC still covers insurance and all maintenance and repairs.
Double Net (NN)
Peach Block lease
Tenant pays property taxes AND insurance. IFC still responsible for maintenance and repairs. The 2024 NOI crater ($449K vs. $888K in 2023) reflects this — $657K in farmland maintenance/repair hit IFC's P&L because of NN exposure.
Triple Net (NNN)
Nut & Fig + Open Ground leases
Tenant pays taxes, insurance, AND maintenance. IFC collects rent and touches nothing. The NNN structure on the nut block through 2032 is a genuine selling point — buyers can underwrite to a clean, unencumbered cash flow stream.
Why this matters for the sell decision: The Parcel A nut block (NNN through 2032) is easier to market than Parcel B (NN through 2029) because a buyer of the nut block has no operating exposure — they are buying a six-year income stream, full stop. A buyer of the peach block is implicitly underwriting IFC's maintenance exposure for the remaining lease term. A sophisticated buyer will discount accordingly, and you should model that discount explicitly when you receive broker opinions of value on each parcel.

The capex rent line ($14,094/yr on the Peach Block) is a separate surcharge IFC is collecting to recoup a specific capital investment made in the orchard — likely drip irrigation infrastructure or a pump upgrade. This is a knowledge gap: whether that infrastructure is owned by IFC and transfers with the parcel, or is owned by the tenant, materially affects both the sale price and the buyer's willingness to renew the lease.
